The First Nation to Reach Another World

The Soviet Union achieved what was once thought impossible, launching humanity's first successful missions to the Moon. Through the Luna programme, Soviet scientists and engineers pioneered lunar exploration, achieving numerous historic firsts that paved the way for all future space exploration.

From the first impact on the lunar surface to the first soft landing, from the first images of the far side to the first robotic sample return missions — the Soviet space program wrote the opening chapters of humanity's journey to the Moon.

Historic Missions of the Luna Programme

First Impact

Luna 2

September 14, 1959

The first spacecraft to reach the surface of the Moon, Luna 2 impacted in the Mare Imbrium region, carrying Soviet pennants to the lunar surface. This historic achievement proved that objects from Earth could reach another celestial body.

★ First human-made object to reach another celestial body
Far Side

Luna 3

October 7, 1959

Luna 3 conducted the first-ever photographic mission to image the far side of the Moon, revealing terrain never before seen by human eyes. The mission returned 29 images covering about 70% of the lunar far side.

★ First images of the lunar far side
Soft Landing

Luna 9

February 3, 1966

Achieving the first controlled soft landing on the Moon, Luna 9 transmitted panoramic photographs from the lunar surface. The mission demonstrated that spacecraft could safely land on the Moon without sinking into the regolith.

★ First successful soft landing on another world
Lunar Orbit

Luna 10

April 3, 1966

Luna 10 became the first spacecraft to achieve lunar orbit, conducting scientific measurements and studying the Moon's magnetic field, radiation environment, and gravitational anomalies.

★ First artificial satellite of the Moon
Sample Return

Luna 16

September 24, 1970

The first robotic mission to return samples from another celestial body. Luna 16 soft-landed in Mare Fecunditatis, collected 101 grams of lunar soil, and autonomously returned it to Earth.

★ First automated sample return mission
Lunokhod

Luna 17

November 17, 1970

Delivered Lunokhod 1, the first successful robotic rover on another world. The eight-wheeled rover operated for 11 months, traveled 10.5 km, transmitted over 20,000 images, and conducted extensive soil analysis.

★ First planetary rover mission
Sample Return

Luna 20

February 25, 1972

Landed in the lunar highlands north of Mare Fecunditatis and returned 55 grams of lunar samples. This mission demonstrated the capability to land and return samples from more challenging terrain.

★ Highlands sample return success
Lunokhod

Luna 21

January 15, 1973

Deployed Lunokhod 2, an improved rover that traveled 37 km (a record that stood for 40 years) and operated for 4 months. It transmitted thousands of images and conducted detailed geological surveys.

★ Longest distance traveled by a lunar rover (until 2014)
Sample Return

Luna 24

August 18, 1976

The final mission of the Luna programme successfully drilled 2 meters into the lunar surface and returned 170 grams of samples, including the first deep core sample. This marked the end of an era of Soviet lunar exploration.

★ First deep drill core sample from the Moon

Timeline of Soviet Lunar Achievements

1959

The Beginning of Lunar Exploration

Luna 1 becomes the first spacecraft to reach the vicinity of the Moon and enter heliocentric orbit. Luna 2 achieves first impact. Luna 3 photographs the far side.

1966

Mastering Soft Landing

Luna 9 achieves the first soft landing on the Moon, while Luna 10 becomes the first artificial satellite of another celestial body.

1970

Automated Sample Return Era Begins

Luna 16 successfully returns lunar samples using a robotic spacecraft. Luna 17 deploys Lunokhod 1, the first planetary rover.

1971-1973

Continued Scientific Exploration

Multiple Luna missions conduct orbital surveys, sample returns from different geological regions, and rover operations with Lunokhod 2.

1976

Final Mission Success

Luna 24 performs the last Soviet lunar mission, successfully drilling and returning the deepest core sample from the Moon's surface.

The achievements of the Soviet Luna programme remain fundamental to our understanding of the Moon.

These missions demonstrated that robotic exploration could accomplish complex scientific objectives.
